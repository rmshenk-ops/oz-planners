Field Reference

Section 01

Key Terms & Concepts

Core vocabulary for working with Opportunity Zones 2.0, development finance, and the planning tools most relevant under OBBBA.

Tax Incentive Programs

Opportunity Zones
OZ / QOZ
Low-income census tracts designated for increased private investment through capital gains tax relief. Established by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017. Now permanent under OBBBA — redesignated every ten years. Must be below 70% of median state or metro area income (down from 80% under OZ 1.0).
Updated by OBBBA
Opportunity Funds
QOF — Qualified Opportunity Fund
Investment vehicles, organized as corporations or partnerships, that must hold at least 90% of assets in qualified OZ property. Investors roll capital gains into these funds within 180 days of a gain event. The fund delivers tax deferral, step-up in basis, and potential full elimination of gains after 10 years.
Permanent
Step-Up in Basis
The core tax benefit mechanism
The reduction in taxable capital gains achieved by holding an OZ investment over time. Under OZ 2.0: 10% step-up in urban OZs after 5 years; 30% step-up in rural OZs after 5 years. After 10 years: 100% step-up in all OZs, gains on the OZ investment are fully eliminated.
Rural bonus new in OZ 2.0
Low-Income Housing Tax Credit
LIHTC
Dollar-for-dollar federal tax credit for affordable housing development, established 1986. OBBBA permanently increased 9% allocations by 12% and lowered the bond financing threshold from 50% to 25%, the largest LIHTC expansion in decades. Estimated to finance 1.22 million new affordable homes by 2035.
Significantly expanded
New Markets Tax Credit
NMTC
A 39% federal tax credit over seven years for investments through Community Development Entities (CDEs) in low-income communities. Made permanent by OBBBA at $5 billion per year, no longer subject to periodic reauthorization. Key gap-filling tool for commercial, mixed-use, and community facility projects.
Now permanent
Historic Tax Credit
HTC / IRC §47
A 20% federal income tax credit for rehabilitation of certified historic structures. Administered by the National Park Service and IRS. Was NOT eliminated or modified by OBBBA, remains fully available and is an active capital stacking partner for OZ and NMTC deals. H.R. 2941 (2025) proposes enhancements.
Unchanged — still available

Finance Structures

Capital Stacking
The strategic layering of multiple financing sources — senior debt, mezzanine debt, preferred equity, common equity, grants, and tax credits — each with its own risk and return profile. Each layer is repaid in priority order. Community development projects often require 6–10 funding sources assembled simultaneously.
Public-Private Partnership
P3 (in practice: P3+)
A financing structure combining multiple levels of public funding (federal, state, local) with private capital. Built from tax credits, grants, foundation dollars, bond issuance, revolving loans, and other sources. Called P3+ because successful community development deals require far more than two partners.
CDFI
Community Development Financial Institution
A mission-driven lender certified by the U.S. Treasury that provides below-market debt to fill capital gaps in low-income communities. CDFIs can offer flexible terms conventional lenders cannot because their investors prioritize community impact. Key gap-fill partner for OZ equity stacks, provides the debt layer that QOFs cannot.
On-Balance Sheet Loan Fund
A lending structure where the institution holds loans on its own books, bears the default risk, and can offer below-market rates and flexible terms. Contrasts with off-balance sheet structures where loans are packaged and sold. Allows a mission-driven lender to fill gaps that conventional banks decline.

Organizational Structures

Public Benefit Corporation
PBC
A for-profit legal entity chartered to pursue both financial returns and a defined social mission. Directors are legally protected, and required, to balance profit against community outcomes without being sued by shareholders. Enables raising private equity capital while maintaining mission guardrails. OPAL Investments, BC is this structure.
Nonprofit Family of Companies
A model where a 501(c)(3) nonprofit serves as parent, with for-profit and mission-driven subsidiaries underneath. Allows full capital stack assembly under one mission-aligned roof. The OPAL model: nonprofit parent → public benefit corporation (equity) → registered investment adviser + CDFI loan fund (debt).

Planning Concepts

Place-Based Community Development
Strategies tailored to a specific geography that leverage local strengths, assets, and community priorities rather than applying top-down solutions. Emphasizes collaboration with actual residents and stakeholders. Opportunity Zones are a place-based policy instrument. The question is whether investment serves the place's existing residents.
Placemaking
A people-centered approach to planning public spaces that prioritizes community engagement, local identity, and well-being. Integrates cultural, social, and economic dimensions. Transforms underutilized areas into vibrant, inclusive places. Core principle: the community is the expert.
Community Prospectus
An EPA-proposed seven-step planning document to guide sustainable and equitable OZ investment: (1) mobilize stakeholders, (2) define community goals, (3) identify assets and challenges, (4) prioritize projects, (5) identify investment strategies, (6) identify financing, (7) market the prospectus to investors.
The Dead Zone
The implementation gap between OZ 1.0 (TCJA 2017) and OZ 2.0 (OBBBA 2025). New designations take effect January 1, 2027. This window is an opportunity — not a problem — for municipalities to prepare census tracts, build stakeholder networks, and create investable project pipelines before capital begins to flow.
Opportunity window: now–Jan 2027

Section 03

Action Steps for Planners

Near-term actions your municipality can take now — before new OZ designations take effect January 1, 2027. These steps build the foundation for investment-ready census tracts regardless of whether your community is ultimately designated.

The window is now

The "dead zone" between OZ 1.0 expiration and OZ 2.0 designation is an opportunity, not a gap. Communities that use this time to prepare will be positioned to attract investment the moment new designations are announced. Those that wait will compete for investor attention from a standing start.

  • 1
    Map your qualifying census tracts
    Identify which census tracts in your city or region are likely to qualify under OZ 2.0 income thresholds (below 70% of median state or metro area income, or 20% poverty rate with 125% of median income). Begin building a picture of what investment those tracts could absorb.
    HUD OZ Mapping Tool Census Bureau ACS Data Novogradac OZ Map
  • 2
    Conduct a community prospectus process
    Use the EPA's seven-step community prospectus framework to document your community's goals, assets, challenges, and investable projects. This document educates potential investors and demonstrates community readiness — it is the planner's most direct tool for shaping what investment looks like.
    EPA Community Prospectus Guide Stakeholder engagement Asset mapping
  • 3
    Remove barriers to investment
    Developers are acutely sensitive to project delay costs. Accelerated permitting processes and reduced or eliminated parking minimums are two of the highest-leverage actions a municipality can take. Every month shaved from a permitting timeline and every parking space eliminated from a project requirement directly improves developer feasibility calculations.
    Permitting reform Parking minimum reduction In-lieu parking fees Sustainable dev code
  • 4
    Build your stakeholder network
    Identify and connect with your local CDFI, regional foundations, state housing finance agency, economic development partners, Main Street program, and any CDEs active in your area. OZ projects are assembled from many funding sources — the relationships that make capital stacking possible take time to build. Start now.
    Local CDFI State housing finance agency Regional foundations NMTC CDEs Main Street Alabama / America
  • 5
    Identify and prepare investable projects
    Review your comprehensive plan and existing city assets for redevelopment potential. Identify underutilized properties, vacant buildings with historic potential, infill development sites, and community facilities needing capital. Work with the PDAP (Property Development Assistance Program) model to move projects from concept to construction-ready.
    Comprehensive plan review Historic property inventory Infill site mapping Pro forma development
  • 6
    Evaluate your capital stack options
    For each priority project, evaluate which combination of financing tools applies: OZ equity, LIHTC, NMTC, HTC, state revolving funds, Tax Increment Financing, foundation grants, and CDFI gap financing. The planning matrix (see Matrix tab) guides which tools are most applicable by place type. No single tool is sufficient alone.
    OZ equity LIHTC (9% or 4%) NMTC Historic Tax Credits TIF CDFI gap debt
  • 7
    Advocate for model legislation
    Support state and federal legislation that strengthens the equity of OZ 2.0: reinstatement of Historic Tax Credit enhancements, Green and Resilient Retrofit funding, Neighborhood Access and Equity grants, Environmental and Climate Justice Block Grants. Also advocate for nonprofit direct payment provisions that allow community ownership stakes within OZ capital stacks.
    H.R. 2941 (HTC enhancement) Community ownership provisions State tax credit alignment

Section 04

Post-OBBBA Tool Status

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (signed July 4, 2025) significantly changed the development finance landscape. This table reflects the current status of key tools as of April 2026.

Net assessment

The core community development finance toolkit is more stable post-OBBBA than many analysts initially feared. OZs, LIHTC, NMTC, and HTC are all permanent and in some cases strengthened. The real loss is on the green building and clean energy side — which creates a genuine challenge for planners trying to build sustainable, resilient projects and will require creative financing solutions at the local level.

Sustainability challenge

The elimination of green building incentives creates a direct tension with equitable and sustainable OZ development. Planners should advocate for local and state green financing tools to fill the gap, and explore whether district-scale solutions (geothermal, waste heat, community solar) can substitute for individual building incentives that no longer exist federally.

Section 05

The OPAL Model

Opportunity Alabama (OPAL) in Birmingham, AL, is the most cited OZ 1.0 success story and the most replicable model for planners. It placed Alabama in the top ten for OZ investment nationally by building a mission-driven organizational structure that could assemble the full capital stack internally.

Opportunity Alabama, Inc. 501(c)(3) nonprofit parent · Founded 2018 · Birmingham, AL

+ OPAL Advisory, LLC

Why this structure works

OPAL can assemble the full capital stack — OZ equity, HTC equity, mezzanine debt, and below-market CDFI gap financing — through a single relationship with a mission-aligned organization. For a developer working in distressed communities, this dramatically reduces transaction costs and closes deals that would otherwise fall apart assembling six to eight separate funding sources. The nonprofit parent provides mission accountability; the PBC subsidiary enables profit-seeking equity; the CDFI fills the gap no conventional lender will touch.

Key Lessons for Planners

  • 1
    Local investment unlocks national capital
    Alabama Power, Regions Bank, and local philanthropic entities were the founding partners of Opportunity Alabama. Their local credibility and commitment signaled to national investors that the organization and its projects were real. National OZ capital follows credible local institutions — it rarely leads.
  • 2
    The CDFI fills the gap the QOF cannot
    OZ equity is structurally required to be equity — it cannot be structured as a low-interest loan from the fund itself. But a CDFI operating alongside the OZ fund can provide the below-market debt layer that fills match requirements for state revolving funds, federal grants, and other programs requiring local match. This is the structural innovation OPAL embodies.
  • 3
    Technical assistance is as important as capital
    OPAL's Property Development Assistance Program (PDAP) helps emerging developers navigate pre-development planning, budgeting, pro forma creation, and capital stacking — before any money is committed. Getting projects to investable condition is where planners and CDFIs add the most value, because capital cannot flow to projects that aren't ready to receive it.
Impact to date

OPAL has facilitated $600M+ in new investment across 40 Alabama communities, developing 1.5M+ square feet of real estate. Their board includes representation from Regions Bank, Alabama Power, and the Economic Development Partnership of Alabama — demonstrating the institutional anchoring that makes mission-driven investment vehicles credible to national capital markets.

Can this model be replicated?

Yes — with the right local anchors. The OPAL model requires: (1) a founding nonprofit with credible mission, (2) corporate and philanthropic anchor partners willing to provide initial capital and board leadership, (3) a CDFI certification or partnership with an existing CDFI, and (4) a pipeline of investable projects. The organizational structure itself is replicable in any state. The harder work is building the local relationships and project pipeline that make investors confident enough to commit.

About This Research

Unpacking Opportunity Zones 2.0 for Planners
Capstone Paper · GWU Sustainable Urban Planning Program · August 2025 · Updated April 2026
This paper investigates the changes to Opportunity Zones under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act and proposes near-term actions planners can take to make census tracts investment-ready in a sustainable and equitable way. Drawing on gray literature and case studies from Birmingham AL, Erie PA, Dolton IL, and Buckhannon WV, the paper develops a planning matrix and model legislation proposals aimed at ensuring OZ 2.0 investment serves existing residents.
